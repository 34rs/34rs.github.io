Labour Unions (in more detail)

Labour unions are organizations formed by a group of workers who come together to negotiate for better worker’s rights. For example, in Canada, there are labour unions like:

  • CUPE (Canadian Union of Public Employees)
  • UFCW (United Food and Commercial Workers) (Canada and US)
  • Teamsters Union / International Brotherhood of Teamsters (Canada and US)
Labour unions’ main purpose is to negotiate for better wages, better working conditions, and/or better benefits. There are pros and cons to labour unions for both employees and employers—for example, employees can negotiate for better wages, while employers can maintain a streamlined form of negotiation with all employees. However, it is also harder to fire unionized employees who are not doing their job. Likewise, labour unions put more pressure on employers to make changes to satisfy employees.

In our organizational behaviour class, we learned about many recent labour union disputes, such as the one between Canadian Pacific Railway (CPR) employees, unionized by Teamsters, and the Canadian Pacific Railway management. We learned that CPR management wanted to cut worker’s benefits, so the workers went on strike. By banding together, the CPR employees cut a key vein of the Canadian economy, forcing the government/CPR management to respond. This likely would not have happened if it was just a few non-unionized employees. By learning about labour unions, Ayden and I have developed a better understanding of future careers/jobs. We will now know to weigh the pros/cons of a unionized vs non-unionized job.

If you look around you right now, you are under a unionized corporation providing quality education under the facade of a school that is falling apart.
